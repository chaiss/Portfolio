About

I've spent eight years in content design, much of it in regulated industries, and four before that in technical writing, building documentation and courseware for complex 3D engineering software. I work best in environments where getting content wrong has real consequences for real people.

I've built content design practices from scratch as the sole content designer in large organisations, shaped design systems' voice guidelines, and partnered with research, product, and engineering teams to deliver end-to-end digital experiences.

The problem hiding in plain sight

The support team at Teachers Health had a workaround. When members called asking how to complete basic tasks in the portal or app, staff would manually email them step-by-step instructions. Not because help content didn't exist, but because what existed was a tangle of roughly 30 FAQs scattered across the site, written with outdated steps, inaccurate details, and no clear structure. Members weren't failing because the product was broken. They were failing because the content was.

As the content designer for the Digital Member Experience team, I came in during active platform transformation, with features releasing to the portal and app at different times. Before I could build anything new, I had to understand what was actually true.

Discovery: fact-checking, not just auditing

I audited every FAQ, but I didn't stop there. I worked through the production environments myself, testing flows and checking whether the help content matched what actually existed in the portal and app. Many FAQs were outdated or described features that had since changed. I used the call-driver data to understand which tasks were generating the most support contacts, cross-referenced it with engagement analytics, and went directly to product and marketing teams to verify facts wherever I wasn't sure. The content couldn't just be clear. It had to be accurate.

One finding shaped everything: marketing had established "Online Member Services" as the brand name for the portal, but members used at least eight different names for it. "The website," "my account," "the portal," "My Teachers Health." The gap between brand terminology and member language was everywhere. Rather than rename it, I added real member language throughout the guides to meet them where they were.

Strategy: get buy-in first, build second

A full overhaul wasn't realistic. There were marketing stakeholders who owned parts of the site, a legal review process, and a feature release calendar that wasn't going to wait. So I proposed a phased approach: start with a landing page and six task-based how-to guides, scoped to the top tasks members were calling about, accessible from the mega menu. I presented this to the marketing stakeholders directly, explained the structure and the rationale, and got their buy-in before writing a word. The task-based structure was a deliberate choice over a searchable knowledge base. The FAQs were already searchable. The problem was they were unstructured and messy. What members needed was a clear path that matched what they were trying to do. Restructuring the searchable FAQ section itself was a far bigger job, a full website redesign and information architecture rebuild, and members couldn't wait a year for it. The guides delivered help now.

Design in Figma, write in Word, publish in Umbraco

I designed the landing page and guide structure directly in Figma, not because it was in my job description, but because it was the fastest way to get the Digital MX team and designers aligned on structure and content hierarchy before writing began. Teachers Health didn't have a web design system at that point, so I used native Figma components to mock up the pages. Content reviews happened in Word, with Figma screens shared alongside for reference. Then I published in Umbraco, hitting the deadline ahead of a portal feature release that would have made the existing FAQ content immediately outdated.

While the guides project was in flight, I was also writing and reviewing app and portal copy in parallel, working closely with legal on every word. That relationship mattered.

Separately, I established the content foundations the team had never had: a digital writing guide, a terminology glossary, a RACI, and a ways of working document for review and publication, all within my first four months.

Outcomes

  • Roughly 30 fragmented FAQs consolidated into 6 task-based how-to guides, grounded in call-driver data and member language research
  • Landing page designed in Figma, written, reviewed, and published in Umbraco ahead of a feature release deadline
  • Every guide fact-checked against live production environments before publication
  • App and portal copy written and reviewed with legal simultaneously
  • Style guide, glossary, RACI, and content review process established for the first time
  • Microsoft Copilot agents built for tone and terminology review, shared across the team

UniSuper · 2025
Members shouldn't need to call for this: making an insurance claim goes digital
Content Design UX Writing User Research Content Strategy WCAG 2.2 Figma Word
+

A phone call that shouldn't have to happen

Every time a UniSuper member needed to make an insurance claim, they had to call. No digital path, no way to check eligibility, no way to understand the process without picking up the phone. For members dealing with illness, injury, or the death of a loved one, that call was one more thing to manage at the worst possible time.

The brief: build a greenfield digital claims experience that let members start a claim without calling. But claims content sits at the intersection of legal obligation, emotional sensitivity, and task clarity. Get any one of those wrong and you either breach compliance, distress the member, or leave them confused about what comes next. Good microcopy is a form of care, and nowhere does it matter more.

Before a wireframe: getting the language right

The hardest work happened before any design tool was opened. The claims support team had their own internal language for every stage of the process. What they called a "pre-assessment phase," members would need to understand as an "eligibility check." These weren't cosmetic differences. They changed how members understood what was being asked of them and what came next. I mapped every term, ran content workshops, and audited existing customer-facing communications before a single Figma frame existed.

Then I wrote storyframes: content-first outlines of the experience that established tone, structure, and key decisions. This was a deliberate choice. Content added late to an existing design gets squeezed into whatever space is left. Content that shapes the design from the start is a different thing entirely.

Designing with research, not after it

I wrote the briefs for every user research session and attended the interviews, so findings fed directly into content decisions rather than arriving as a summary weeks later. Research confirmed the calibration I'd been writing toward: the right amount of empathy, carried by professional clarity. Warm enough to feel supported, never pretending to be a best mate.

"When I'm talking to my super fund, I don't want a best mate. I want you to be professional about it."

"It was more fact based. I think the empathy and compassion needs to come from the people on the phone."

Knowing this going in was one thing. Having research confirm it made it inarguable to stakeholders.

The review process problem

When designs were ready for review by the claims support team, a challenge emerged fast. Figma was hard for them to navigate. They were time-poor. Reviewing copy in Word while cross-referencing Figma screens wasn't working. Rather than push the format, I moved to multiple in-person review sessions, working through content together and resolving terminology questions in real time. It was slower. But it was the only approach that actually worked for this team.

Outcomes

  • Members going through illness, injury, or loss can now check their eligibility and start a claim without sitting on hold, being triaged, and re-explaining a painful circumstance to another stranger
  • Greenfield digital claims experience released May 2026: phone-only to fully digital
  • Storyframes delivered before wireframing began, keeping content at the centre of design
  • Internal claims terminology resolved into clear member-facing language before a screen was built
  • Tone calibrated from user research: the right amount of empathy, delivered with professional clarity
  • WCAG 2.2 and regulatory compliance championed throughout

The problem nobody owned

Across Atlassian's product suite, "Learn more" links were failing users who relied on screen readers. A screen reader navigating a page with five "Learn more" links reads each one without context. The user has no idea where any of them lead. It's the content equivalent of a door with no sign. This wasn't a Bitbucket problem. It was an Atlassian-wide problem. But because it lived in content rather than code, it had no formal owner.

I made it mine.

From a Bitbucket audit to a design system standard

I researched the WCAG guidelines, audited the current state across products, and created a DACI framework in Confluence to map who needed to be involved across design, engineering, and content. Rather than quietly fix it in Bitbucket and move on, I built a cross-functional initiative to resolve it systematically. A patch in one product while the same pattern persists in six others isn't a solution. The fix was descriptive, contextual link text. It was tested, refined through feedback, and ultimately embedded in the Atlassian Design System as the permanent standard for all future product development.

Outcomes

  • Systemic accessibility gap identified and resolved across all Atlassian products
  • Initiative led end to end: from audit to design system standard
  • New link copy guidelines permanently embedded in the Atlassian Design System
  • Direct improvement for users relying on assistive technology
  • Reduced cognitive load for all users, not just those with visual impairments

A platform with ambition and a content problem

NorthOnline is AMP's adviser-facing wrap platform: a tool for financial advisers to design and build client investment portfolios, manage accounts, and access AMP's product suite in one place. The goal was bold: become the fastest-growing wrap platform in Australia by 2025. But the site had been built without a UX writer. Content had been squeezed into design templates by whoever was available, key tasks were buried, navigation labels didn't match how advisers thought about their work, and the site was sitting at position 35 on average in search.

The brief landed with less than eight weeks of UX runway. The question wasn't whether to do research. It was whether there was enough time to do it properly. There was, but only if the research directly drove the content decisions rather than informing them in a separate phase.

Running research and letting the data lead

I led two rounds of tree testing with 11 advisers via Optimal Workshop, and two rounds of user interviews via Askable. The tree testing results were clear: global navigation needed to move to the main navigation, and headings needed a fundamental rethink for findability and comprehension. Stakeholders had assumptions about how advisers navigated the product. The data told a different story, and there was no debating it.

Running this research also taught me things worth carrying forward. Bringing in a subject matter expert made the interviews sharper. Refining methods between rounds kept A/B testing on track. And a working group retrospective surfaced a structural gap: the team needed a design system and aligned content templates, which had contributed to the inconsistency in the first place.

Content, SEO, and publishing in one sprint

With the IA decisions made, I co-designed Figma content templates with the UX team, rewrote copy to fit the new structure, and ran SEO keyword optimisation using Google Keyword Planner. I worked alongside a producer in Adobe Experience Manager to make sure the HTML structure and content were correct before publishing, learning the platform as the project progressed.

What it's actually like

A few things worth knowing before you reach out.

🍩

Donuts, occasionally

In one role I was writing and reviewing app copy, portal copy, and six self-service guides, all at once, all with legal. We'd be reviewing every word I wrote together under a tight deadline, so starting well was worth the investment. Collab sessions get them too. I want people to show up, feel welcome, and have fun doing serious work. I'll still push back on a word choice. But we'll do it over donuts.

👋

First to put my hand up

No designer available? I'll mock up the screens. No design system for web? I'll build the Figma components. No cross-team alignment process? I'll run the fortnightly meetings. I've done all three across different organisations, not because I was asked, but because it was clearly what the work needed.

📖

Genuine content nerd

I own content design books. I go to content meetups. I've run content channels in my roles and I've been known to drop a well-timed content joke into Teams. I get genuinely excited about a well-structured error message and the difference a single word can make.

🌟

I love helping people

Whether it's a junior designer navigating their first stakeholder review, a product team who doesn't know they need a content strategist yet, or a legal team that thinks plain language will compromise their obligations: I'll meet you where you are.

I'm into volunteering, painting and drawing, all dogs, brewing beer, and commuting via cycling. People who are interesting outside of work tend to bring something different to it. I try to be one of those people. And occasionally, I bring donuts.
