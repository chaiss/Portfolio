The content design unicorn:
what the job description really means

It seems like more organisations are looking for a content designer who can do it all. Fewer can explain what "it all" actually means.

Christine Haiss
Senior Content Designer · June 2026

Content in most organisations is split across two worlds that rarely talk to each other. In my experience, they only start talking when a content designer builds the bridge between them.

Marketing owns the brand voice. Campaigns, announcements, content that builds awareness and drives consideration. Success looks like reach and engagement. The audience is browsing, considering, learning. You have their attention because they chose to give it.

Product needs something different. User interface (UI) copy, error messages, onboarding flows, self-service guides. Content that helps someone complete a task under stress, often in a regulated environment where a wrong word has real consequences. Success looks like task completion and fewer support calls. The audience isn't browsing. They're doing something, deciding something, or urgently needing help.

These two disciplines require different training, different instincts, and different ways of measuring value. But the market is increasingly asking for one person who can move fluently between both.

What "do it all" looks like in a job ad

And that person (if you find them) also needs to bring search engine optimisation (SEO) strategy and content management system (CMS) publishing capability to the table. The result's a job description that looks something like this: content designer who can write UI copy (the same work ads variously call UX writing, microcopy, or copywriting for app), develop content strategy, optimise for search engines and now for AI answer engines, publish in the CMS, read the analytics, meet accessibility standards, and align with brand voice. Regulated industry experience highly regarded, sometimes required: government, insurance, finance, superannuation. Oh, and the role is a single hire.

That list isn't speculative. It's drawn from real job ads and interviews I've recently experienced. The titles vary. The ask doesn't. And it keeps growing, because discovery itself is changing. Search engines are becoming answer engines, and generative engine optimisation (GEO) is starting to appear alongside SEO, ahead of most organisations even knowing to ask for it.

Here's how I think about the difference

Marketing & Comms Digital Content Design
Primary goal Inform, promote, build brand Help users complete a task
Audience mindset Browsing, considering, learning Doing, deciding, needing help
Tone Brand voice, engaging, persuasive Clear, direct, reassuring
Format Articles, campaigns, announcements UI copy, error messages, guides
Success measure Reach, engagement, brand perception Task completion, self-service, reduced support calls
Lives in Website, email, social, print App, portal, product interfaces
Works with Marketing, brand, PR Product, design, engineering, legal
Discoverability Keyword strategy, campaign SEO Search intent, GEO, findable help content
Publishing CMS, email platforms, social tools CMS, design systems, production pipelines

Discoverability and publishing are the odd ones out. They don't sit in either world. They run across both, which is exactly why organisations keep attaching them to whoever owns the content. SEO, GEO, analytics, and the CMS aren't a third discipline. They're the connective tissue between the two that already exist.

The judgement is the job

Understanding which mode you're in, and switching deliberately, is the skill organisations are actually looking for. Which is worth keeping in proportion: the centre of this role is the craft. Knowing what to say, to whom, in what moment, and what success looks like when you're done. Search and publishing capabilities matter, but they're extensions of that judgement, not substitutes for it. A designer who understands search intent already understands their reader. A designer who has structured content in one system can structure it in any of them. The tools transfer because the judgement is what's doing the work.

Here's a small proof of how low the tooling bar has dropped. This article lives on a hand-built page, coded with AI assistance and shipped through GitHub Pages in a weekend. Knowing what the page should say, who it's for, and why the structure works the way it does took twelve years. The tools have never been the hard part.

The unicorn isn't a mythical creature. But they're rare, because the combination takes years to build across genuinely different environments: regulated industries where compliance reviews every word, research that's allowed to change your mind, years inside design teams learning the craft from the other side of the Figma file, publishing at midnight before a launch, metadata for a page nobody was finding.

So that's what "do it all" actually means. Not a checklist of tools. The judgement to know which world you're writing in, the craft to do it well, and the range to carry content across both: discoverable, accurate, published, measured. Two worlds that rarely talk to each other. The unicorn is the one who gets them talking.
